Does ESG Investing
Pay Off in India?

A quantitative analysis of ESG vs Non-ESG stock performance
across 120 NSE-listed companies · January 2022 – January 2025

Krithika Das

CHRIST University, Bangalore

Research Verdict
Non-ESG stocks outperformed ESG stocks by 3.5% annually (16.3% vs 12.8%) over the study period — but ESG stocks were significantly less volatile (25.3% vs 32.3%). On a risk-adjusted basis, Sharpe ratios were nearly identical (0.252 vs 0.296), and the return gap is not statistically significant (p = 0.198). ESG investing in India does not hurt returns — it trades raw upside for a smoother, more consistent ride.

Key Findings
Finding 01
Non-ESG stocks delivered higher raw returns — but at a cost
Non-ESG stocks outperformed by 3.5% annually (16.3% vs 12.8%). The Non-ESG universe was dominated by high-performing PSU stocks — BEL (+56.5%), REC (+47.4%), PFC (+46.7%), Canara Bank (+44.1%) — which ran hard on government capex tailwinds. These are high-beta, policy-driven plays that don't screen well on ESG criteria but delivered outsized returns in this specific cycle.
Non-ESG top performer: BEL at +56.5% annualised
Finding 02
ESG stocks were dramatically less volatile
The 7% volatility gap (25.3% vs 32.3%) is the most practically significant finding. For an investor who needs to sleep at night — or a fund with drawdown limits — ESG stocks offered a meaningfully smoother ride. The return distribution box plot shows ESG returns tightly clustered around the median, while Non-ESG had far more extreme outliers on both sides.
ESG volatility 21.8% lower than Non-ESG on relative basis
Finding 03
Risk-adjusted returns were essentially equal
The Sharpe ratio gap of 0.044 (0.252 vs 0.296) is economically negligible. This is the most honest summary of the data: once you account for the extra risk Non-ESG investors took on, both groups performed almost identically. You were not rewarded for taking on the additional volatility of Non-ESG stocks on a per-unit-of-risk basis.
Sharpe gap: 0.044 — within normal statistical noise
Finding 04
The return gap is statistically inconclusive
A p-value of 0.198 means there is a 19.8% probability the observed gap is purely random. Academic convention requires p < 0.05 to declare a finding significant. We cannot conclude that ESG stocks genuinely underperform — the sample size (60 per group) and 3-year window are simply not large enough to make a definitive claim either way.
p = 0.198 · Cannot reject null hypothesis at 95% confidence
Finding 05
ESG stocks had a higher "win rate"
83.3% of ESG stocks delivered positive returns vs 78.3% of Non-ESG. ESG investing in India appears to reduce the probability of picking a loser, even if it reduces the magnitude of the biggest winners. For a conservative investor building a long-term portfolio, this consistency matters more than chasing the top performers.
5% more ESG stocks delivered positive returns
Finding 06
ESG losers were concentrated in FMCG & IT
The bottom ESG performers — Wipro (−6.2%), Asian Paints (−4.3%), LTIM (−4.1%), Dabur (−2.5%) — are predominantly quality FMCG and IT names that de-rated as rate hikes compressed multiples globally. These are not ESG failures — they're valuation mean-reversions in richly priced sectors that happened to dominate the ESG index.
Bottom ESG performers: Wipro, Asian Paints, LTIM, Dabur

Notable Observation
PSU dominance in Non-ESG: 4 of the top 5 Non-ESG performers are PSU companies (BEL, REC, PFC, Canara Bank) that benefited from India's government capex supercycle and defence spending. These stocks are excluded from ESG indices due to governance concerns — which explains much of the raw return gap. This is a cycle-specific phenomenon, not a structural ESG underperformance.

ESG losers are valuation stories: Wipro, Asian Paints, and Dabur de-rated on stretched valuations — not ESG-specific issues. They would have underperformed in any non-ESG index too.

Methodology
Step 01
Universe Construction
ESG group: 64 Nifty100 ESG Index constituents. Benchmark: additional 64 Nifty100 non-ESG companies. After removing delisted/unavailable tickers: 60 ESG + 60 Non-ESG = 120 total.
Step 02
Data Collection
3 years of daily adjusted closing prices pulled via yfinance (Yahoo Finance API). Period: Jan 2022 – Jan 2025. Auto-adjusted for splits and dividends.
Step 03
Return Computation
Annualised return computed as (1 + cumulative return)^(1/years) − 1. Volatility = annualised std dev of daily returns × √252. Sharpe = (Annual Return − 6.5%) / Volatility.
Step 04
Statistical Testing
Independent samples T-test (scipy.stats) on annual returns to assess whether the ESG/Non-ESG return gap is statistically significant. Significance threshold: p < 0.05.
Step 05
Tools Used
Python 3 · pandas · matplotlib · seaborn · scipy · yfinance. Built in Google Colab. All code available on GitHub.
Step 06
Risk-Free Rate
6.5% used as the Indian risk-free rate — approximate yield on 10-year Government of India bonds as of the study period. Used for Sharpe ratio computation only.
06
Limitations & Caveats
Honest Acknowledgements
  • Sample size of 60 per group limits statistical power — a larger dataset would strengthen conclusions
  • 3-year window (2022–2025) captures a specific market cycle dominated by PSU stocks — results may not generalise
  • Survivorship bias: delisted companies excluded from data, which may inflate returns for both groups
  • ESG scores sourced from NSE index composition, not individual Bloomberg/MSCI ESG ratings — a cruder proxy
  • Equal weighting used — a market-cap weighted approach may yield different results
  • Does not account for sector composition differences between ESG and Non-ESG groups
  • Price returns only — dividend yields not separately analysed (could favour PSU/Non-ESG stocks)
  • p = 0.198 means we cannot definitively conclude ESG underperforms — results are directional, not conclusive
